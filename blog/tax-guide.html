Freelance Tax Guide 2026

Published June 2026 · Freelance Finance · 8 min read

"I didn't know I had to pay quarterly taxes. Now I owe $12,000 in penalties."

That's what a freelancer told me in February. They'd been freelancing for 2 years and never paid estimated taxes. The IRS doesn't care that you didn't know.

Here's everything you need to know about freelance taxes in 2026.

📊 Self-Employment Tax

When you're employed, your employer pays half your Social Security and Medicare taxes (7.65%). When you're freelance, you pay both halves — 15.3% on your net earnings.

This is called self-employment tax, and it's in addition to your income tax.

💡 Pro tip: You can deduct the employer half (7.65%) from your income. It's not a huge deduction, but every dollar helps.

💰 Quarterly Estimated Taxes

You must pay taxes quarterly if you expect to owe $1,000+ in taxes for the year. The due dates are:

  • Q1: April 15
  • Q2: June 15
  • Q3: September 15
  • Q4: January 15 (of the next year)
⚠️ Warning: Miss a quarterly payment and you'll owe penalties. The penalty is roughly 0.5% per month on the underpayment.

How Much to Pay

The safe harbor rule: pay 100% of last year's tax liability (or 110% if you earn $150K+). This protects you from penalties even if you earn more this year.

Or estimate your actual income and pay 25% each quarter. But that's risky if you have a big quarter.

📝 Tax Deductions for Freelancers

Track everything. Here are the most common deductions:

  • Home office: Percentage of rent/mortgage, utilities, internet
  • Equipment: Computer, monitor, phone, software subscriptions
  • Professional development: Courses, books, conferences
  • Health insurance: 100% deductible if self-employed
  • Retirement contributions: SEP-IRA or Solo 401(k)
  • Business meals: 50% deductible (be careful with documentation)
  • Travel: Business travel is fully deductible
  • Professional services: Accountant, lawyer, consultant fees
💡 Pro tip: Use a separate business credit card for ALL business expenses. It makes tracking automatic.

🏛️ Business Structure

Sole Proprietorship (Default)

No setup required. You're the business. Simple, but no liability protection. Your personal assets are at risk.

LLC

Limited liability protection. Costs $50-$500 to form (varies by state). You can elect S-Corp status later.

S-Corp

Potential tax savings if you earn $60K+. You pay yourself a "reasonable salary" and take the rest as distributions (no self-employment tax on distributions).

⚠️ Warning: S-Corp has additional paperwork and payroll requirements. Not worth it under $60K/year.

📅 Annual Tax Calendar

  • January: Send 1099s to contractors you paid $600+
  • March: File taxes (or extension) if you have a partnership/LLC
  • April: Personal tax return due + Q1 estimated taxes
  • June: Q2 estimated taxes
  • September: Q3 estimated taxes
  • October: Extended tax return due (if you filed an extension)
  • January (next year): Q4 estimated taxes
